## 3. Your Ideal Client

**Describe the person you most want to walk through your door:**

They're usually between 28 and 50. Professionals. Often in tech, medicine, law, startups, or academia. They're high-functioning on the outside. From the outside, their life looks fine. Maybe even enviable. But internally they're running on fumes and they know it.

They've probably done therapy before. Maybe a few rounds. It helped, to a point. But they feel like they keep circling the same patterns: the anxiety that never fully resolves, the relationship dynamic they can't seem to break, the burnout they recover from only to recreate. They're not looking for someone to validate them. They're looking for someone who will help them understand why they keep ending up here.

They're smart, introspective, and a little skeptical of anything that feels too soft or scripted. They want a therapist who will be real with them. They value directness. They can handle hard feedback. They want depth, not coping strategies.

When they search for a therapist, they're feeling some combination of frustrated, exhausted, and ready. Ready to do something different. Ready to go deeper than they've gone before.

**What are they Googling?**

- "EMDR therapist Oakland"
- "anxiety therapist near me" (from East Bay locations)
- "trauma therapist Oakland CA"
- "therapist for burnout Bay Area"
- "EMDR for anxiety"
- "therapist for high achievers"
- "somatic therapy Oakland"
- "therapist who does EMDR and IFS"
- "why does therapy not work" (this one's real, and I want to speak to it)

**What do they need to feel in the first 10 seconds?**

Calm. Not the fake, stock-photo-of-a-waterfall kind of calm. Actual visual calm. Clean space, confident language, no clutter. They need to feel like the person behind this site knows what they're doing. They need to feel like the site was designed with the same care and intentionality they'd expect from the therapy itself. If the site feels thrown together, they'll assume the therapy is too.

Also: they need to see themselves. Not literally, but the copy needs to signal "I understand the kind of person you are" within the first few lines. If they're a burned-out product manager or a physician who can't sleep, they need to feel like I get that world.

**Who is NOT a good fit?**

- People in active crisis who need a higher level of care (I'll help them find the right resource, but I'm not an emergency service)
- Anyone looking primarily for medication management (I'll refer to a psychiatrist)
- Couples (I do individual work on relationship patterns, but not couples therapy. I have referrals for that)
- Clients under 18
- People who want a therapist who will mostly listen and reflect back. I'm interactive and direct. If someone wants a quiet holding space, I'm not the right fit, and that's completely fine.

## 5. How You Work / Therapeutic Approach

**Modalities I use:**

- EMDR (I'm EMDRIA Certified, not just basic trained. This is a meaningful distinction and I want it on the site.)
- IFS (Internal Family Systems). Completed Level 1 and Level 2 training through the IFS Institute.
- Hakomi (somatic, mindfulness-based). I bring body awareness into almost every session.
- Attachment-based and relational framework underneath everything.
- I draw on psychodynamic thinking but wouldn't call myself a psychodynamic therapist.

**Which to highlight on the site:**

EMDR first. It's my lead modality and my strongest SEO angle. IFS second. Somatic/Hakomi third. I'd like to explain how they work together rather than just listing them as separate things.

**In plain language, what's it like to be in a session with me?**

It's not what most people expect. I don't sit in silence waiting for you to fill the space. I'm engaged. I ask questions. Sometimes pointed ones. I'll notice when your jaw tightens or your breathing changes and I'll name it. Not to make you uncomfortable, but because your body often knows something your mind hasn't caught up to yet.

We'll talk, but we'll also work. If you're processing a traumatic memory, we might use EMDR, which involves following a light bar or tapping while letting your brain reprocess the memory. It sounds unusual and it is. It's also one of the most effective trauma treatments that exists. If we're exploring a pattern, I might use parts work (IFS) to help you understand the different parts of you that are in conflict. If something comes up somatically, we'll slow down and pay attention to what your body is telling us.

I'm warm, but I'm not soft. I'll challenge you. I'll also make you laugh sometimes. I think therapy should be rigorous, not precious.

**What's distinctive about my approach?**

A few things. First, I integrate multiple modalities in a way that's tailored rather than formulaic. A lot of therapists are trained in EMDR or IFS. Fewer know how to weave them together with somatic awareness in a way that follows the client's process rather than following a protocol.

Second, I came from tech. I understand the culture my clients live in. I'm not going to tell a product manager to "set better boundaries" without understanding the organizational dynamics that make that nearly impossible. I've been in those rooms.

Third, I'm direct. Bay Area therapy culture can lean toward a very gentle, open-ended style. That works for some people. My clients tend to be the ones who tried that and felt like they were paying $250 to hear "and how does that make you feel?" They want someone who will push back, name what they see, and move the work forward.

## 6. Your Story

**Why did you become a therapist?**

I didn't plan to. For six years I was a UX researcher at a mid-size tech company in the South Bay. I was good at the work. I understood how people think, what they need, how to design systems that actually serve them. But the culture was eating me alive. I was having panic attacks before stakeholder meetings and telling myself it was normal. I was sleeping four hours a night and calling it discipline.

I started therapy at 28. It was the first time anyone had asked me what I actually felt rather than what I thought I should feel. That question changed my life. Not overnight. But it opened a door I couldn't close.

Two years later I left tech and enrolled at CIIS. My colleagues thought I was having a breakdown. I think I was having a breakthrough.

I went into community mental health first. Spent two years at a clinic in East Oakland working with people in crisis, people dealing with poverty and violence and systemic trauma. It humbled me. It also gave me a clinical foundation I couldn't have gotten anywhere else. I learned to stay steady in the room when things got heavy. I learned that healing isn't linear and that people are more resilient than they know.

I started my private practice in 2018. Most of my clients are some version of who I used to be: accomplished, anxious, running on fumes, and ready to figure out why.

**Something about you outside of work:**

I run trails. Redwood Regional Park, Joaquin Miller, Tilden. It's not a wellness practice for me. It's how I stay sane. I live in Oakland with my partner and a deeply opinionated rescue mutt named Hank. I read more fiction than self-help. I cook elaborate meals and never follow the recipe.

**Personal connection to your specialties:**

Burnout, obviously. I didn't just study it. I lived it. And the anxiety that comes with high-achiever culture. The need to perform, to be perceived as competent, to hold it all together at the cost of your actual wellbeing. I know that experience from the inside.

I also understand the specific moment when therapy shifts from "this is helping me cope" to "this is changing something fundamental." That shift is what I want to facilitate for my clients.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

**Questions new clients commonly ask:**

- How is EMDR different from talk therapy?
- How long does therapy take?
- How often do we meet?
- What happens in the first session?
- Do I have to talk about my trauma in detail?
- What if I cry? (I love this question. It tells me so much about someone.)
- How do I know if we're a good fit?
- What's your cancellation policy?
- Do you take insurance?
- What's the difference between EMDR, IFS, and somatic therapy? How do you decide what to use?

**Misconceptions you correct often:**

- "EMDR is just following a light with your eyes." It's a comprehensive protocol with eight phases. The bilateral stimulation is one piece.
- "If I don't have a big-T trauma, I'm not a good candidate for EMDR." EMDR is effective for anxiety, negative beliefs, and smaller but impactful experiences too.
- "Therapy means lying on a couch and talking about your childhood for years." My approach is more active and targeted than that.
- "I should be able to handle this on my own." This one is pervasive, especially with high-achievers. Seeking help is not weakness. It's efficiency.

**Process details to address upfront:**

First session: We'll talk about what brought you here, your history, and what you're hoping to get from therapy. I'll explain how I work and we'll start figuring out if we're a good match. No EMDR in the first session. We build a foundation first.

Frequency: I typically see clients weekly. For EMDR processing, I sometimes recommend twice a week during active processing phases.

Duration: It depends on what we're working on. Some clients come for 3-6 months of focused EMDR work on a specific issue. Others stay longer for deeper relational and attachment work. I don't believe in therapy lasting forever. I want you to not need me.
